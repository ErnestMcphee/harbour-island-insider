Pink Sand Beach:
The Complete Visitor's Guide

🏖️
By Aaliyah Johnson, Resort Concierge
· March 28, 2026 · 5 min read
🏖️

When most people picture a tropical beach, they picture white sand. Harbour Island throws that image out the window entirely. The famous Pink Sand Beach — stretching three unbroken miles along the Atlantic side of the island — is exactly what the name promises: blush-pink, powdery, and utterly surreal.

I've been welcoming guests to this beach for twelve years as the resort's concierge, and I still catch myself stopping to stare. Here is everything you need to know to make the most of it.

Why Is the Sand Pink?

The short answer: foraminifera. These microscopic single-celled organisms live in the coral reef just offshore and have bright red or pink shells. When they die, their shells wash ashore and mix with the white calcium carbonate sand, turning it that distinctive rosy hue.

The longer the foraminifera shells have been there and the more tightly packed the beach, the deeper the pink. At Harbour Island, the colour ranges from a barely-there blush in dry, bright midday light to a rich, warm rose in the low golden light of morning and evening.

"I've been to beaches on six continents. Nothing prepared me for the colour of this sand. My phone camera simply couldn't do it justice."

— Priya W., Toronto, 5-star review

Best Time to Visit

The beach is beautiful year-round, but certain times make it extraordinary:

  • Sunrise (6:30–8:00am): The beach faces east, so it gets the first light of day. The low angle of the sun turns the sand a deep, saturated pink — and you'll almost certainly have it to yourself. This is the photographer's hour.
  • Late afternoon (4:00–6:00pm): The light softens, the day-trippers thin out, and the Atlantic takes on a brilliant turquoise that sets off the sand perfectly.
  • Avoid midday on weekends (December–April): Day-trip boats from Nassau arrive around 10am and leave by 3pm. If you want space, arrive early or go late.

In terms of seasons, the Bahamas is genuinely pleasant year-round. The driest and calmest months are December through April (peak season). May through November brings more clouds and occasional squalls but also far fewer crowds and significantly lower rates at the resort.

Getting to the Beach from Valentines

From Valentines Marina and Resort, Pink Sand Beach is a pleasant 12–15 minute walk across the island. The route takes you through the heart of Dunmore Town — past colourful colonial cottages, the Queen's Highway, and a few tempting bakeries if you started early.

Alternatively, our complimentary golf-cart shuttles run every 30 minutes starting at 7am. Ask at the front desk or marina office for the current schedule. You can also rent your own golf cart from several operators in town for around $60/day.

🚗 Golf Cart Tip

If you rent a golf cart, drive south along the beach to find the quieter, less-visited stretches. The further you go from the public access points, the more likely you are to have a long, empty stretch entirely to yourself.

What to Bring

  • Sunscreen (reef-safe only): The Bahamas has banned oxybenzone and octinoxate sunscreens to protect the coral. Bring mineral sunscreen (zinc oxide or titanium dioxide) — or buy some at our marina store.
  • Snorkel gear: The reef just offshore is alive with colour and fish. There's decent snorkelling right off the beach from about waist depth. We rent full sets at the dive centre.
  • Water and snacks: There are a handful of beach bars and shacks along the beach, but they have limited hours. Bring your own water — the Bahamian sun is serious business.
  • A camera with a good lens: Phone cameras struggle with the subtle pink in bright light. Try shooting in portrait mode or with a warm filter in post-processing.
  • A beach umbrella: There's very little natural shade. We loan umbrellas and beach chairs to resort guests — just ask.

Swimming & Ocean Conditions

The Atlantic side can be energetic. Most of the time, the surf is a gentle 1–2 feet and the beach is perfectly safe for swimming, including with children. However:

  • There are no lifeguards on the beach. Swim aware.
  • After a cold front (October–March), the surf can build to 4–6 feet quickly. Check with our concierge or dive staff for a current conditions update each morning.
  • The current can run strongly north or south along the beach. Don't swim too far out, and make sure children stay in the shallows during any swell.
  • Jellyfish are occasionally present, particularly in spring. Your first clue will be others getting out of the water.

Beach Etiquette & Conservation

This beach is a natural wonder and a living ecosystem. A few requests from us and from Harbour Island's community:

  • Take nothing but photographs and memories. No shells, no sand, no sea glass (it's illegal to export natural materials from the Bahamas).
  • Reef-safe sunscreen only. Conventional sunscreens bleach and kill coral.
  • Leave the beach cleaner than you found it. Carry out what you carry in.
  • Give nesting sea turtles (May–October) a wide berth — stay at least 30 feet away and do not use lights or flash at night.
  • No motorized vehicles on the beach.

Near the Beach: Dunmore Town

The beach runs the full length of the island, and Dunmore Town — the main settlement — sits just a short walk inland. Make time to explore: the colourful colonial architecture, the tiny churches, the art galleries tucked between local bars, and the legendary Sip Sip restaurant overlooking the Atlantic.

My personal recommendation: arrive at sunrise, watch the light show on the sand, have breakfast at one of the beach bars when they open around 9am, then stroll into town before the heat of midday. Head back to the resort for lunch and a nap, then return in the late afternoon when the light is magic again.

However you time it — the Pink Sand Beach will leave its mark on you. In thirty-plus years of welcoming guests to Harbour Island, I've never met anyone who regretted coming. 🌸
