Island Life

25 Things to Do on
Harbour Island (Beyond the Beach)

🌴
By Aaliyah Johnson, Island Life & Travel Editor
· April 18, 2026 · 9 min read
🌴

Every first-time visitor comes for the Pink Sand Beach. This is correct and understandable — three miles of blush-coloured sand and turquoise Atlantic is not something you pass on. But the visitors who fall hardest for Harbour Island are the ones who discover that the beach is just the beginning. This is an island with excellent food, serious diving, historic architecture, exceptional fishing, and an atmosphere that exists nowhere else in the Bahamas.

Here are 25 things to do on Harbour Island — enough to fill a week, though honest regulars will tell you a week is never quite enough.

01

Sunrise on Pink Sand Beach

The beach faces east, catching the first light of day in a way that turns the sand a deep, saturated rose. Set your alarm, walk across the island before anyone else is awake, and stand at the water's edge as the sun clears the horizon. It will be the best 45 minutes of your trip. Read our complete guide to Pink Sand Beach for timing and what to bring.

02

Drift Dive at Current Cut

Current Cut is one of the most thrilling dives in the Bahamas — a narrow channel between Eleuthera and Current Island where the tidal current runs at up to 6 knots, carrying you through at speed past sharks, eagle rays, and dense schools of fish. You simply drop in and let the ocean do the work. Valentines Dive Centre runs regular trips to Current Cut and will match you with the right tide window for the conditions.

03

Rent a Golf Cart and Explore End to End

No cars on Harbour Island — it's golf carts all the way. Rent one for the day ($60–80 from operators in Dunmore Town) and drive the full length of the island: north past the historic cemetery, south along the Atlantic-side track, across to the harbour and back. The island is small enough to cover completely in a few hours, but rich enough in small details to fill an entire day of slow exploration.

04

Eat Cracked Conch at a Local Shack

Conch is the taste of the Bahamas, and cracked conch — pounded, breaded, and fried until golden — is the preparation that most locals prefer. Skip the tourist versions and find the local shacks near the government dock or beach access points. Order a plate with peas and rice, plantains if they have them, and a cold Kalik. This is the meal that explains why people come back.

05

Watch the Sunset from the Valentines Marina Bar

The western (harbour) side of Harbour Island faces Eleuthera across the shallows, and the sunsets over that calm water are a nightly event. The marina bar at Valentines is the prime viewing position — cold drinks in hand, the boats rocking gently at the docks, the sky doing something extraordinary. No agenda required. Show up, order a Goombay Smash, and watch.

Don't Miss

Sunset at Valentines Marina Bar

The marina bar at Valentines Resort offers the best sunset views on the harbour side of the island — right on the water, with the boats rocking in the foreground and the sky changing by the minute. Whether you're a resort guest, docked in a slip, or just stopping by for a drink, you're welcome. Reserve a room or slip and make every evening this good.

06

Bone Fish on the Eleuthera Flats

The tidal flats between Harbour Island and Eleuthera are serious bonefish water — shallow, sandy, gin-clear, with fish that are notoriously spooky and powerful. Fly fishing for bonefish on the flats is one of the definitive sporting experiences in the Bahamas. The Valentines concierge can arrange half-day and full-day charters with experienced local guides who know exactly where the fish are running.

07

Snorkel the Reef from Pink Sand Beach

The coral reef runs just offshore along the Atlantic side of the island, reachable from the beach in waist-deep water. The reef is alive — angelfish, parrotfish, sergeant majors, the occasional nurse shark resting on the bottom. Bring reef-safe mineral sunscreen (conventional sunscreen is banned in the Bahamas), a mask and fins, and follow the colour. Valentines Dive Centre rents full snorkel kits.

08

Walk Historic Dunmore Town

Dunmore Town is the oldest settlement in the Bahamas — founded in the 1780s by British Loyalists fleeing the American Revolution. The evidence of that history is still visible in the architecture: wooden colonial cottages painted in vivid Caribbean colours, stone walls built centuries ago, narrow lanes that pre-date the golf cart by 200 years. Walk slowly. The details reward attention.

09

Visit the Island's Oldest Church

St. John's Anglican Church on Dunmore Street dates to 1768, making it one of the oldest churches in the Bahamas. The building is modest and beautiful, surrounded by a cemetery with gravestones worn smooth by two and a half centuries of Atlantic weather. Even non-religious visitors find it quietly moving — a physical reminder of how long human life has rooted itself to this tiny island.

10

Day Trip to Spanish Wells

Spanish Wells is a small, tight-knit community of primarily white Bahamian fishermen on St. George's Cay, a 20-minute ferry from North Eleuthera. It is one of the most interesting communities in the Bahamas — prosperous, orderly, deeply rooted in its fishing heritage, and architecturally quite distinct from anywhere else in the islands. It's worth a half-day excursion. Ask at the marina about ferry schedules.

11

Get Your PADI Open Water Certification

Valentines Dive Centre offers full PADI Open Water certification courses — the standard entry-level scuba qualification that opens up the entire world of diving. The course takes approximately three days: pool sessions, theory (now done via online e-learning before you arrive), and four open-water dives on the reef. The instructors are experienced, patient, and deeply familiar with the local sites. Read our guide to diving Harbour Island for the full picture of what's in the water here.

12

Buy Fresh Fish Direct from the Fishermen

The government dock in the afternoon is where the fishing boats come in and the day's catch goes on sale. Snapper, grouper, triggerfish, whatever's running — bought straight from the fishermen, cleaned on request, priced as fish should be. If you have access to a kitchen (villa guests and liveaboard boaters especially), this is the best meal you'll cook all trip.

13

Take the Water Taxi to the Glass Window Bridge

The Glass Window Bridge on mainland Eleuthera is one of the most dramatic natural features in the Bahamas — a narrow rock ridge (now bridged by road) where the dark blue Atlantic and the turquoise Bight of Eleuthera are separated by just a few feet of land. During winter swells, waves crash over the road in spectacular fashion. Take the water taxi to North Eleuthera and arrange a taxi to the bridge — about 30 minutes south. Combine it with a stop at Surfer's Beach if the swell is running.

14

Join a Harbour Island Regatta

Harbour Island hosts sailing regattas in June and again in December — traditional working boat racing that is one of the great spectator sports in the Bahamas. The wooden sloops are raced hard by crews who take this seriously. The social scene around the regatta is warm, loud, and inclusive of visitors who show genuine interest. Ask at the marina for dates and the best viewing spots.

15

Deep-Sea Fishing Charter

The deep water beyond the Eleuthera shelf holds wahoo, mahi-mahi, tuna, and marlin. A half-day or full-day offshore charter is the way to access it. The Valentines concierge can arrange charters with local captains who know the fishing grounds well — speak to the desk the day before, as charters often book up and the boats go out early.

16

Kayak or Paddleboard in the Protected Harbour

The harbour side of Harbour Island is shallow, calm, and clear — perfect for paddling. The water is typically knee-depth to waist-depth across the flats, and on a clear day you can see the sandy bottom and occasional rays gliding beneath you. Valentines has kayaks and paddleboards available for resort guests. Head out in the early morning before the afternoon wind picks up.

17

Explore the Dunmore Town Cemetery

The old cemetery on the edge of Dunmore Town contains gravestones dating back to the late 1700s. The inscriptions are a compressed social history of the island — Loyalist settlers, freed slaves, fishermen, merchants, a physician or two. The oldest stones are barely legible; the newer ones tell stories in the Bahamian tradition of elaborate epitaphs. It's a genuinely interesting hour for anyone with a sense of history.

18

Dine at Valentine's Restaurant for the Waterfront Sunset Experience

This deserves a separate entry from the marina bar. Valentine's Restaurant — the proper dining room at Valentines Resort — is the finest sit-down dinner experience on the island. Reserve a table on the waterfront terrace for 6:30pm in winter, order the fresh-caught fish of the day and the cracked conch, and watch the harbour light change through the meal. If you're docked in a marina slip or staying in a villa, you walk straight to dinner. This is what the resort was built around. Make a reservation.

19

Night Dive with Valentines Dive Centre

Valentines Dive Centre runs night dives on Tuesday and Thursday evenings, and the reef at night is a completely different experience from the daytime — octopus hunting, sleeping fish within arm's reach, bioluminescence in the water column, and the particular stillness of the deep in the dark. These dives run small groups; book ahead at the dive centre desk. Appropriate for certified divers of all experience levels.

20

Learn to Cook Bahamian Food

Bahamian cooking — conch fritters, peas and rice, baked grouper, guava duff — is a regional cuisine in the proper sense, distinct from anywhere else in the Caribbean. Several local women on the island give informal cooking lessons; the Valentines concierge can arrange an introduction. You'll spend a morning in a local kitchen, eat extremely well, and go home with the recipes. This is one of the more memorable things you can do on the island.

21

Visit the Art Galleries in Dunmore Town

Harbour Island has attracted artists for decades — drawn by the light, the colours, and the isolation that makes it possible to work. The galleries are small and scattered through the town; some are barely marked, found only by wandering. The work ranges from naive Bahamian folk painting to serious contemporary work by artists who have built studios on the island. Ask locals to point you toward what's open.

22

Photograph the Pink Sand in the Morning Golden Hour

The Pink Sand Beach in the hour after sunrise is the most photogenic thing on this island, and this island is not short of photogenic things. The low-angle light, the saturated pink of the wet sand, the Atlantic turning from dark indigo to clear turquoise as the sun rises — it is a genuinely extraordinary combination. Phone cameras struggle with the subtle colour in bright light; shoot in portrait mode or shoot RAW and adjust warmth and saturation in post. Go at sunrise.

23

Take the Ferry to Eleuthera and Explore by Car

Eleuthera is 110 miles long and contains enough to fill several days of exploration. Take the water taxi to North Eleuthera, rent a car (arrange in advance — rental options are limited), and drive south: the Glass Window Bridge, the Ocean Hole at Rock Sound, the historic settlement of Tarpum Bay with its hand-painted buildings, the wild south coast. It's a long day but a genuinely rewarding one. Come back to Harbour Island in the late afternoon before the ferry schedule winds down.

24

Stargazing on the Beach

There is essentially no light pollution on Harbour Island at night. The Pink Sand Beach, with its long Atlantic exposure and dark horizon, is as good a stargazing location as almost anywhere in the Caribbean. The Milky Way is visible on clear, moonless nights — a dense, textured band across the sky in a way that most people who grew up near cities have genuinely never seen. Bring a blanket, lie on the sand, and look up for half an hour.

25

Come Back Next Year

It happens to almost everyone who comes to Harbour Island. Not just "I'd like to return someday" but an active, specific plan formed somewhere around day three or four: same time next year, same place, longer this time. The island does something to people. It's a combination of the scale (small enough to feel known within a few days), the beauty (relentless, in the best way), and the particular atmosphere of an island that hasn't been packaged or over-developed. You'll understand when you get here.
